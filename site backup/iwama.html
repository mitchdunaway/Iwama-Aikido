Iwama

The entrance to the Dojo from the road .



Iwama is a small country village a set in a quiet farming area about 1 hour and 30mins train ride north-east of Tokyo on the Mito line. It is famous in the Aikido world for being the home the Aiki-jinja (the Aikido shrine), and the Iwama Dojo, which served as a home to O-Sensei when he moved from Tokyo to the country in 1942. Apparently it was a dream of O-Sensei to lead a relaxed and healthy country life, mixing farming with Martial Arts training. Here he was able to develop his ideas into the art we know today. The Iwama Dojo is one of the few remaining traditional "old style" training centres in Japan. It is set in roughly 17 acres of beautiful oak forest and remains practically the same as it was 50 years ago
The dojo

The Iwama Dojo, with O-Sensei's house attached, to the left

. The Dojo is small; only a 50 mat floor space, yet people from all over the world come to train with Saito Sensei, and experience the tough life and vigorous training needed to temper the Martial Spirit
Training is twice a day, morning and evening, Monday to Saturday. Students can live at the Dojo as "Uchi-deshi", (inside students), or as the locals and the longer term foreigners usually do, train as "Soto-deshi" (outside students). The uchi deshi cook their own meals, do the chores around the Dojo and immerse themselves in the training life of traditional Budo, by receiving the favoured teaching of Saito Sensei. Extra training is also undertaken when University groups come to Iwama for special intensive periods, so students could be training anything up to 4- hours a day. Training is tough, and discipline is strict; students are expected to conform to the traditional Japanese way, this not only develops great inner strength, but also builds solid character.More importantly, students who have spent time at Iwama exhibit a strength and sharpness of technique that is characteristic of such intense study, and develop into teachers of the highest calibre.
The Aiki-jinja


The Aiki-jinja, or Aikido Shrine, at Iwama

The Aikido shrine, which occupies a plot of land on the other side of the road to the dojo, was actually completed before the dojo, in 1944. It was built to O-Sensei's plan based on principles of Kotodama, utilising it's component forms; the triangle, circle and square. It enshrines 43 deities, to whom O-Sensei prayed almost every day. In 1962, a Ceremonial Hall in front of the shrine was completed, and that is what the visitor sees today. Since O-Sensei's passing in 1969, both the Shrine and the Dojo have been left in the care of Saito Sensei. Regular Ceremonies are held at the Shrine, with the present Doshu and visitors from Tokyo coming to join in rememberence of O-Sensei and other important Shinto dates.
The small structure to the left is a natural spring, which produces cool and fresh water. Students claim that the taste of the water is different from day to day. Weapons classes are often conducted on the grounds in front of the hall.
Morihiro Saito Sensei
Head of Iwama Dojo and keeper of the Aiki-jinja
Morihiro Saito Sensei, 9th Dan, is the longest serving student of O-Sensei, having trained with him for 23 years, and has been the head of the Iwama dojo since O-Sensei's passing. Since then, Aikido has grown in popularity, and students from around the world come to Iwama to train with Saito Sensei and experience the life of traditional Martial Arts training.

Saito Sensei, now 70, has been training Aikido for more than 50 years, and is regarded as the world's technical expert on Aikido. He is the author of a highly acclaimed series of reference training manuals, and travels around the world conducting seminars.

Saito Sensei was born near Iwama, and was a railway worker for many of his earlier years. He originally came from a karate background, and had done some kendo, as so many Aikido practioners do. In those early years, Saito Sensei was famous for his strength and vigorous training method. Stories of him travelling to Tokyo and picking fights with gangsters, to test the Aikido techniques he learnt are told in the same breath as praise for his ability as a teacher providing clear, systematic and easy to understand instruction.

Now that Saito Sensei is in his 70's, it is evident that his Aikido has changed, yet older students at the seminars still report that his throws "still have that power", and his footwork is just as fast and precise as ever.

Saito Sensei appreciates that he will not be in this world forever, and so has asked many of his top students, who are now instructors with their own dojo around the world, to be active in preserving the Martial Art of O-Sensei. At the most recent seminar in Sydney, he spoke of some who believe weapons are not necessary in Aikido, and others who have begun to change the art to suit their own means. Saito Sensei believes that what O-Sensei left the world is a valuable legacy, and would like to see it preserved for future generations.

This is the undertaking of the Takemusu Aiki Association.

Shinto Shrine on Atago Mountain
I wama is situated at the foot of Atago Mountain, which commands an impressive view of the surrounding countryside. On occassions, the Aikido deshi would run through town and up the several hundred metres of road to the top of the mountain, where there is a Shinto Shrine, and priests quarters. Here, in the grounds before the shrine, weapons classes are sometimes held, and it is the backdrop for the cover and several of the outdoor photos in Saito Sensei's first volume of the Traditional Aikido series. The shrine is dedicated to the local fire god, a powerful deity of Shinto belief. Homage and sacrifice is made to pacify the deity with the hope of preventing bushfires in the neighboring region, which relies on farming and agriculture for it's prosperity.

Atago Mountain is also famous in the Zen tradition, for it was here that the Zen priest Takuan rode a horse up and down the shrine's stone steps to demonstrate to his friend Lord Yagyu Tajima no kami the Zen principles of mental obscurities blocking physical action. Lord Yagyu, upon remembering the famous horseman Magaki Heikuro riding up and down the stairs at Atago on horseback, was unable to perform the feat himself. Takuan rode the stairs easily, saying "there is no horseman on horseback, nor horse under the saddle."
